Personal Statement

My residency training and subsequent fellowship in pediatric anesthesiology allowed me to develop clinical expertise and a strong research interest in the effects of early anesthesia in infants and children. In addition, I completed a Masters Degree in Clinical Research Methods from my institutional NIH/CTSA-funded Clinical Research Training Program and recently earned a KL2 Mentored Career Development Award. My research focuses on clarifying the neurophysiologic effects of clinical anesthesia in human infants. In doing so, I hope to contribute to the knowledge of the brain response to clinical anesthesia early in human development. To this end, we are enrolling infants presenting for indicated general and spinal anesthesia and recording the full-head montage, unprocessed electroencephalogram (EEG). We already have a dataset of 54 infants receiving sevoflurane anesthesia and have published our preliminary analyses. We now have preliminary data in 25 infants receiving spinal anesthesia. The proposed research project aims to characterize the clinical sedation state associated with spinal anesthesia in early age and clarify its neurophysiologic correlates. We aim to: (i) recruit the entire sample size, (ii) record video-EEG before anesthesia and after spinal anesthesia administration, (iii) perform clinical-behavioral assessments of the sedated state using a validated, quantitative tool (the Pediatric Sedation State Scale), and (iv) analyze EEG data from this and age-matched controls during physiologic sleep and sevoflurane general anesthesia using power spectral analysis and other techniques. The results from this work will form the basis of future prospective studies of anesthesia during early age in a K23 application. My ultimate goal is to become an independent clinical investigator.

Contribution to Science

During COVID, I led an observational study of pediatric patients admitted to our health system in the heart of the pandemic in New York City. The preliminary data from this study was published and led to a grant award funded by the Albert Einstein College of Medicine’s Dean’s Office to study the epidemiology of COVID-19 in pediatric patients. I have also authored a case report describing pediatric cerebellar mutism syndrome, an important complication after posterior fossa tumor resection. I have collaborated on quantitative reviews examining the role of regional anesthesia on cognitive function as well as persistent postoperative pain in adults and children published in the Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews. I have authored a narrative review with collaborators examining the important role of simulation in pediatric sedation training.
